Question 10: Energy to raise temperature

How much energy is needed to raise the temperature of 100.0 g of tungsten by 10°C?

Given:

  • Mass (m): 100.0 g
  • Specific heat capacity (c): 0.13 J/g·°C
  • Temperature change (ΔT): 10°C

Formula: Q = m ⋅ c ⋅ ΔT

Calculation:
Q = 100.0 g × 0.13 J/g·°C × 10 °C
Q = 130 J

Answer: 130 Joules of energy are needed.

Question 11: Moles from atoms

How many moles contain 9.50 × 10²⁴ atoms?

Given:

  • Number of atoms: 9.50 × 10²⁴
  • Avogadro’s number: 6.022 × 10²³ atoms/mol

Formula: Moles = Number of atoms / Avogadro’s number

Calculation:
Moles = (9.50 × 10²⁴ atoms) / (6.022 × 10²³ atoms/mol)
Moles ≈ 15.7755... mol

Answer: Approximately 15.78 moles of tungsten contain 9.50 × 10²⁴ atoms.

Question 12: Most common charge(s) of tungsten in an ionic compound

Discussion:
Tungsten most commonly forms compounds with a 6+ oxidation state (such as in tungstates like Na₂WO₄). It can also form compounds with a 4+ oxidation state, but these are less common. Pure ionic compounds of tungsten are rare; most are complex or covalent. For practical purposes (and most classroom settings), the most common ionic-like charge is 6+.

Answer: 6+ (most common charge in ionic-like compounds, such as tungstates)

Full Electron Configuration and Noble Gas Configuration

Full electron configuration:

1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 3d¹⁰ 4s² 4p⁶ 4d¹⁰ 5s² 5p⁶ 4f¹⁴ 5d⁴ 6s²

Noble gas (shorthand) electron configuration:

[Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d⁴ 6s²

Stable Isotopes of Tungsten

Number of common stable isotopes:
Tungsten has five naturally occurring isotopes. Four are considered stable, and one (¹⁸⁰W) has an extremely long half-life and is often included as stable for practical purposes.

Nuclear symbols and hyphen notation:

  • ¹⁸⁰W (tungsten-180)
  • ¹⁸²W (tungsten-182)
  • ¹⁸³W (tungsten-183)
  • ¹⁸⁴W (tungsten-184)
  • ¹⁸⁶W (tungsten-186)

Note: For most classroom settings, all five are considered “stable.” If your teacher wants only the strictly stable isotopes, list the four: ¹⁸²W, ¹⁸³W, ¹⁸⁴W, ¹⁸⁶W.
